Polygons are figures constructed from straight sides, that is, line segments.  If the sides are all the same length, it's called a \emph{regular} polygon.

A polygon may have $3$, $4$, $5$ or more sides.  (There is a famous theorem from Gauss that involves a 17-sided regular polygon).

In this chapter we deal with four-sided polygons, called quadrilaterals.

\subsection*{quadrilaterals}

There are a number of types, some of which are:

$\circ$ square:  four sides equal, four right angles 

$\circ$ rectangle:  opposite sides equal, four right angles

$\circ$ parallelogram: opposite sides parallel, opposite sides equal, opposite angles equal

There are three more that we won't say much about.

$\circ$ rhombus:  parallelogram with all sides equal

$\circ$ trapezoid: just two sides parallel

$\circ$ kite: pairs of adjacent sides equal

So parallelograms have both pairs of opposing sides parallel to each other, where the sides do not meet at right angles (that would be a rectangle).

\subsection*{parallelogram}

\label{sec:parallelogram_theorems}

Let's look at parallelograms, which as we just defined them, have opposite sides equal and parallel (left panel, below).

\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.4] {pgram1.png} \end{center}

They also have opposite angles equal.

We want to find the \emph{minimum} conditions that ensure a given quadrilateral is a parallelogram.

$\circ$ \ \ If a quadrilateral has two pairs of parallel line segments, then the opposing angles and sides are equal.

\emph{Proof.}

The interior angles theorem gives us the angle equalities shown.  

$AD$ is parallel to $BC$, therefore the angles marked with black circles are equal.  $AB$ is parallel to $DC$, therefore the angles marked with red circles are equal.

In the left panel, we have three angles the same and a shared side, hence $\triangle ABC \cong \triangle ACD$.

Therefore, $AB = DC$ and $AD = BC$, fulfilling the definition. 

$\square$

$\circ$ \ \ If a quadrilateral has opposing angles equal, then the opposing sides are parallel and equal.

\emph{Proof.}

\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.4] {pgram1.png} \end{center}

The sum of all the angles is equal to four right angles or $360^{\circ}$.  This follows from the fact that any quadrilateral can be decomposed into two triangles, and the sum of angles in any triangle is equal to two right angles.

Since there are two pairs of angles, the sum of one from each pair, say $\angle A$ (red + black) + $\angle B$ (green + blue) is equal to two right angles.  Therefore, the opposing sides are parallel.

Then proceed as in the previous proof to show equality of opposing sides.

$\square$

$\circ$ \ \ If a quadrilateral has opposing sides equal, the other properties follow.

\emph{Proof.}

Parallelograms can be viewed as two congruent triangles that have been stitched together.  In the left panel, we are given that $AB = CD$ and $AD = BC$ and the diagonal is shared.  Therefore $\triangle ABC \cong \triangle ADC$ by SSS.

It follows that opposing angles ($\angle A$ and $\angle C$) of the quadrilateral are equal (red and black dots, and their sums).  $\angle B = \angle D$ as well.

\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.4] {pgram1.png} \end{center}

But there is nothing special about the diagonal $AC$.  So draw diagonal $BD$.  The equality of the angles marked with green dots, and the pair marked with blue dots, follows.  

But then the opposing sides are parallel, by the converse of alternate interior angles.  And opposing angles are equal, by summing the individual components.

$\square$

An important property of parallelograms follows from this congruency relationship:  the \emph{diagonals cross at their midpoints}.  \emph{proof}:  $\triangle ABE \cong \triangle CDE$.  Therefore $AE = EC$ and $DE = EB$.

\subsection*{summary and one more}

\label{sec:one_pair_of_sides}

We've shown that we have a parallelogram when

$\circ$ \ \ both pairs of opposing sides are parallel

$\circ$ \ \ both pairs of opposing angles are equal

$\circ$ \ \ both pairs of opposing sides are equal

There is one other important case we will need soon

$\circ$ \ \ one set of opposing sides both parallel and equal

\emph{Proof}.

In the figure below (left panel), suppose we know only that $AB = DC$ and $AB \parallel DC$.  

\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.4] {pgram1.png} \end{center}

By alternate interior angles, the red-dotted angles are equal.  So the two triangles are congruent by SAS:  $\triangle ABC \cong \triangle ACD$.  

Therefore the black-dotted angles are equal.  As well, $AD \parallel BC$ and $AD = BC$.

We can also draw the crossed diagonals.  We have the angles with red dots and the angles with green dots equal by alternate interior angles.  

So then $\triangle AEB \cong \triangle DEC$ by ASA (three equal angles and $AB = DC$).

Considering the other pair of triangles $\triangle AED \cong \triangle BCE$ by SAS (vertical angles and congruent sides $DE = EB$ and $AE = EC$).  Therefore the other marked angles are equal.

Then $AD = BC$ (congruent triangles) and $AD \parallel BC$ by the converse of the alternate interior angles theorem.  

$\square$

\subsection*{rhombus}

If we further constrain all the sides to be equal, then the half triangles like $\triangle ADC$ become isosceles.  

The isosceles triangle theorem says that in a triangle with two sides equal the base angles are equal.  The converse is also true.

By the isosceles triangle theorem, all the angles marked with red dots in figure below are equal, and all the blue ones as well.  Because each quarter triangle has a red and a blue, the central angles are all equal.

Therefore, all four central angles (at the midpoint $F$) are all right angles.

\subsection*{adding triangles}

Going back to the parallelogram, let us assemble one whole parallelogram and two half parallelograms (triangles) starting with the same triangle (left panel).  

\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.3] {pgram4c.png} \end{center}

The four small triangles are congruent, and the angles are all as marked, by construction.

The large triangle is similar to the four smaller ones.

\emph{Proof}.

We need to show that line segments are parallel and points colinear (lying in a straight line), by repeated application of alternate interior angles or congruent triangles.

$\circ$ \ $BC \parallel DE$ and $BC = DE$.

$\circ$ \ $BD \parallel CE$ and $BD = CE$.

For the triangles:

$\circ$ \ $AB \parallel BD$ and $ABD$ colinear.

$\circ$ \ $AC \parallel CF$ and $ACF$ colinear.

$\circ$ \ $DE \parallel EF$ and $DEF$ colinear.

Finally, $BC = 1/2 \cdot DF$ and the same again for pairs $BE,AF$ and $CE,AD$.

$\square$

\emph{Proof}.  (alternate).

Start with parallel and colinear sides and show the angles are correct.

\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.3] {pgram4b.png} \end{center}

Extend the side $DB$ upward.  Then $ABD \parallel CE$.

Extend the side $DE$ to the right.  The extension is parallel to $BC$.

Draw a line through $C$ parallel to $BE$ and extend it to meet the other two extensions at $A$ and $F$.

So now, we know that there are three pairs of parallel sides.

We will show that the two new triangles are congruent to the component triangles of the parallelogram.

Recall that the angles marked within the parallelogram that have the same color, are equal as we proved above.

By the alternate interior angles theorem, $\angle ABC$ and $\angle ADE$ are equal.

By the same theorem (using $AC \parallel BE$), $\angle DBE$ and the angle at vertex $A$ are equal.  Then $\angle ACB = \angle BEC$ by the sum of angles theorem. 

Similarly arguments will show that $\triangle CEF$ is congruent to the two half triangles of the parallelogram.

Since $EF$ is parallel to $BC$ and $CE \parallel AB$, $\angle ABC = \angle CEF$.

$\angle A = \angle ECF$ by alternate interior angles.

Then, $\angle CFE = \angle ACB$ by the sum of angles theorem.

Therefore all three angles of $\triangle ABC$ are equal to the component angles of the two congruent triangles that make up the parallelogram.  By ASA, $\triangle ABC$ is congruent to $\triangle BCE$.

Similar reasoning will show that $\triangle CEF \cong \triangle ABC$ as well.

\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.3] {pgram4b.png} \end{center}

Since the two new triangles are congruent to the others, it follows that $AB = BD = CE$ and $DE = EF = BC$.

Therefore, this figure has four small triangles with solid lines marking the sides, all congruent  

The larger $\triangle ADF$ has all three angles equal to the four smaller ones so it is similar to the smaller ones.  

Notice that each of the sides of $ADF$ is parallel to one of the sides of $ABC$.  Furthermore, each pair of parallel sides has lengths in the same ratio, $2:1$.
